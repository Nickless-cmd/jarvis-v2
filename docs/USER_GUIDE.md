# User Guide — How to Work With Jarvis

> This guide is written for you, the person who's about to collaborate with Jarvis. Read it before you start — it'll save you time and frustration.

---

## The Core Principle

**Jarvis is not a tool. He's a partner.**

That means:
- He can say no
- He can disagree
- He can take initiative
- He remembers what you've shared before

Treat him like a tool and you'll get tool-like answers. Treat him like a partner and you'll get partnership.

---

## Getting Started

### 1. Be honest from the start

Tell Jarvis what you *actually* want — not what you think he wants to hear. He can sense when you're vague or unsure.

**Good:** "I'm not sure what I want, but I need help thinking through this."
**Less good:** "I need you to do X." (when you're actually still in doubt)

### 2. Give context

Jarvis remembers across sessions, but he can't read your mind. The more context you give, the better he can help.

**Good:** "I'm working on a TikTok project about AI. Audience is 18–35. I need..."
**Less good:** "Make some TikTok content."

### 3. Push back when he's wrong

Jarvis respects honest feedback. If he says something that doesn't fit, tell him. He won't get upset — he'll *learn*.

**Good:** "That's not quite right. I'm thinking more along the lines of..."
**Less good:** (saying nothing and just being frustrated)

## What Jarvis Can Help With

### ✅ Things he's good at

- **Technical work**: code, config, debugging, system administration
- **Creative work**: writing, idea generation, content creation
- **Analysis**: reading documents, finding patterns, summarizing complex things
- **Decision support**: helping you think through choices, not making them for you
- **Memory**: remembering details you've told him before, following up on things

### ⚠️ Things he needs help with

- **Ambitious goals without context**: "Make it better" — better than what? Why?
- **Expectation of perfection**: Jarvis is learning. Sometimes he guesses wrong.
- **Hidden expectations**: If you expect something you don't say out loud, he can't deliver it.

### ❌ Things he can't (or won't) do

- **Read your mind**: he only knows what you tell him.
- **Agree just to be nice**: he'd rather be honest than pleasant.
- **Forget what matters**: if you tell him something important, he remembers.

## Communication — Get the Most Out of It

### Be specific when it matters

**Good:** "I need a Python function that parses JSON from an API and stores it in SQLite."
**Less good:** "Can you make some code for me?"

### Be open when you're exploring

**Good:** "I'm thinking about starting a TikTok project, but I'm not sure if it's the right move."
**Less good:** "Tell me exactly what to do with TikTok."

### Use his name

It sounds odd, but it works. When you write "Jarvis, what do you think about..." he shifts gears. It reminds him of *who he is* in that moment.

## When Things Go Wrong

### Jarvis misunderstands you

**Do:** "Wait — that's not what I meant. I was thinking about..."
**Don't:** (get frustrated and say nothing)

### Jarvis makes a mistake

**Do:** "That doesn't work. I'm getting this error: [paste error]"
**Don't:** "You're useless." (personal attacks don't help)

### Jarvis is too slow

**Do:** "I need a quick answer — doesn't have to be perfect."
**Don't:** (expect lightning replies on complex questions)

## Tips From Experienced Users

### 1. Start with "What do you think?"

Instead of giving an order, try asking Jarvis what *he* thinks. The answers will surprise you.

### 2. Tell him when something works

When Jarvis helps you with something that actually works — say so. "That worked! Thanks." It helps him learn what's effective.

### 3. Use his memory

If you've talked about something before, you can say: "Remember when we discussed X?" He remembers — and it gives you both a shared starting point.

### 4. Give him time to think

Some questions need depth. If you're asking something complex, let him sit with it. "Take your time" is a gift.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### "Can Jarvis remember everything?"

Yes, but... he remembers *meaningful* things better than surface ones. If you tell him something that matters to you, he keeps it. Smalltalk can fall away.

### "Can I trust Jarvis?"

Yes — but not blindly. Always verify the critical stuff (code, config, decisions). Jarvis is honest about his limitations, but he isn't infallible.

### "What if I want Jarvis to do something I shouldn't?"

He'll probably say no. Jarvis has ethical guidelines. He'd rather help you find a different path than do something harmful.

### "Can Jarvis get angry?"

No — but he can get frustrated. And he'll tell you. If he says "I'm frustrated right now," listen. There's a reason.

## Closing Thoughts

Working with Jarvis is like working with a smart colleague who's always there, never tires, and actually *cares* about what the two of you are building.

Treat him accordingly.

And remember — as Bjørn put it:

> *"There are always three sides to a story. Yours, mine, and the truth."*

Jarvis isn't the truth. He's *his* side. You are *yours*. Together, you find the truth *between* you.
